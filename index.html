What Is Fintech, Really?

"Fintech" has become such a broad term that it's almost useless without a framework. For commercial bankers, it's most helpful to think about fintech through the lens of which banking functions it touches: payments, lending, deposits, treasury management, and compliance.

The companies that matter to you aren't the consumer apps — they're the infrastructure players, the B2B lenders, the treasury platforms, and the embedded finance providers that are quietly inside your clients' ERP systems right now.

Lesson 1.2

Embedded Finance & BaaS

Banking-as-a-Service is arguably the most structurally important trend in fintech for commercial bankers. It allows non-bank companies to offer financial products — deposit accounts, debit cards, lending — by partnering with a sponsor bank that holds the charter.

The implication: your clients' ERP, their fleet management software, their e-commerce platform — all of them could have banking products embedded in them. When that happens, financial activity that might have flowed through your bank flows through a software company instead.

Strategic Question for Your Book: Which of your clients have embedded payment flows or credit products inside their software stack that you don't have visibility into? That's the conversation to be having now, not after they tell you they're moving accounts.

Lesson 2.1

What AI Agents Actually Are

The word "agent" gets attached to everything from a simple chatbot to a fully autonomous trading system. For bankers, precision matters — especially when you're explaining these systems to compliance, presenting to a board, or advising a client who's considering adopting AI tools.

The most useful framework is a spectrum based on autonomy and action scope: how independently does the system operate, and what real-world actions can it take?

Level 1

AI Assistant / Copilot

Responds to queries, drafts content, summarizes documents. A human reviews everything before any action is taken. No autonomy.

Example: Banker asks Claude to summarize a 10-K. Claude provides summary. Banker reads it, decides what to do.

Level 2

Workflow Agent

Executes multi-step tasks with defined boundaries. Can call APIs, retrieve data, and produce outputs — but within guardrails set by humans.

Example: Agent pulls borrower financials from core system, spreads them, flags covenant breaches, sends alert to RM. No credit decision made.

Level 3

Autonomous Agent

Plans, decides, and executes across multiple systems with minimal human checkpoints. Rare in regulated banking — but coming.

Example: Agent monitors portfolio, identifies deteriorating credit, initiates covenant waiver process, schedules client call, prepares briefing — all autonomously.
Banker's Reality Check: Nearly all AI in banking today is Level 1 or early Level 2. Anyone claiming fully autonomous credit decisioning at scale in a regulated bank is almost certainly overstating the case. Know the spectrum so you can ask the right questions.

Lesson 2.3

Compliance & Regulatory Guardrails

Every banker needs a working understanding of the regulatory framework governing AI in banking. You don't need to be a lawyer, but you do need to know the key principles — because your clients will ask about them, your compliance team will reference them, and the questions will only get more pointed as AI use expands.

The Key Document: SR 11-7 — The Federal Reserve and OCC's guidance on Model Risk Management is the foundational framework. It predates modern AI but applies directly: models must be validated, documented, monitored, and their limitations understood. Any AI agent making or influencing credit decisions is a model under SR 11-7.
📋
Model Validation

All AI models used in credit decisions must be independently validated. This includes LLMs used to assist in underwriting analysis.

🔍
Explainability

Adverse action notices under ECOA/Reg B require specific reasons for credit denial. Black-box AI outputs that can't be explained create fair lending exposure.

⚖️
Fair Lending

AI trained on historical data can perpetuate or amplify discriminatory patterns. CFPB has issued guidance making clear that algorithmic decisions are not exempt from fair lending laws.

👤
Human Oversight

For consequential decisions — credit approval, account closure, fraud flags — human review remains a regulatory expectation and best practice even when AI assists.

📁
Data Governance

AI agents often require access to customer data across systems. Proper data governance, lineage tracking, and privacy compliance (GLBA, CCPA) apply fully.

🛡️
Third-Party Risk

Using a vendor's AI model (e.g., OpenAI, Anthropic, Microsoft Copilot) doesn't transfer regulatory responsibility. Banks remain accountable for all model outcomes.

Lesson 2.4

Case Study: JPMorgan's COiN

// Real-World Deployment

Contract Intelligence (COiN) — JPMorgan Chase

Launched in 2017, COiN (Contract Intelligence) was one of the first widely publicized AI deployments by a major bank in a commercial banking context. The system used machine learning to interpret commercial loan agreements and extract critical data points — work that had previously required lawyers and loan officers to review documents manually.

The core task: reviewing 12,000 annual credit agreements to extract roughly 150 attributes per document — things like maturity dates, covenant definitions, collateral descriptions, and pricing grids. Previously this work took roughly 360,000 hours of lawyer and analyst time per year. COiN completed the same work in seconds.

Crucially, COiN didn't replace lawyers — it made them dramatically more productive. The system handled extraction; humans handled interpretation, negotiation, and judgment calls. This is the model that works in regulated environments.

360K Hours saved annually
Seconds vs. months for review
<1% Error rate reported
The Lesson for Your Bank: COiN's success wasn't about replacing expertise — it was about eliminating the mechanical, high-volume work so that expert judgment could be applied where it actually matters. Every commercial bank has equivalent workflows. Loan document review, covenant compliance checking, financial statement spreading — these are your COiN opportunities.

Module 2 — Key Takeaways

AI agents exist on a spectrum — most banking AI today is Level 1 or 2. True autonomy in regulated credit decisions is rare and faces significant compliance hurdles.
The highest-ROI banking deployments are in document-heavy, high-volume workflows: AML monitoring, document extraction, compliance review, and customer onboarding.
SR 11-7 applies to AI models. Any AI used in credit decisions must be validated, documented, and monitored — regardless of the vendor.
Explainability is not just a technical challenge — it's a legal requirement under ECOA/Reg B for adverse credit actions.
The banker's role evolves, not disappears: AI handles extraction and pattern recognition; you provide judgment, relationships, and accountability.

Lesson 3.2

Prompt Engineering for Bankers

The quality of your AI output is almost entirely determined by the quality of your prompt. Vague prompts produce generic output. Specific, structured prompts produce analyst-quality work. Here are the five prompt patterns that consistently deliver the best results for banking tasks.

Pattern 1 — Pre-Call Research Brief
# Copy, fill in the brackets, paste into Claude or ChatGPT You are a senior commercial banking analyst. I have a meeting with [COMPANY NAME], a [INDUSTRY] company with approximately $[REVENUE]M in annual revenue based in [LOCATION]. Please provide: 1. A 3-sentence company overview focusing on their business model and recent strategic developments 2. Key industry headwinds and tailwinds affecting their credit profile right now (cite the specific macro factors) 3. Likely banking needs based on their stage and industry (working capital, capex, M&A, treasury services) 4. Three specific questions I should ask to uncover banking opportunities 5. Any red flags I should investigate before extending credit Format this as a pre-call briefing I can review in 5 minutes.
Pattern 2 — Credit Memo Narrative Draft
# Paste in the borrower's financial summary, then use this prompt You are a senior credit analyst at a commercial bank. Based on the financial information below, draft the narrative section of a credit memo covering: 1. Borrower Overview (2 paragraphs): business description, ownership, management tenure, and competitive positioning 2. Financial Analysis (3 paragraphs): revenue trends, margin performance, leverage, and liquidity — citing specific numbers from the data 3. Strengths & Mitigants (bullet format): 4–5 credit positives 4. Risks & Concerns (bullet format): 4–5 credit risks with mitigants 5. Recommendation setup: one sentence framing the credit decision Tone: professional, direct, no hedging. Write as if for a credit committee that values brevity and specificity. [PASTE FINANCIAL DATA HERE]
The Golden Rule of Banking Prompts: Always tell the AI your role, the audience, the format you need, and the tone. "Write a credit memo" produces garbage. "You are a senior credit analyst. Draft a 500-word borrower overview for a credit committee audience that values brevity. Tone: direct and analytical." produces something usable.

Lesson 3.4

Spreading Financial Statements with AI

Financial statement analysis is one of the most time-intensive tasks in commercial banking — and one of the best candidates for AI assistance. AI won't replace your judgment on what the numbers mean, but it can dramatically accelerate the extraction, calculation, and initial interpretation of key metrics.

Extraction

Lesson 3.5

Portfolio Monitoring & Early Warning

The best time to deal with a problem credit is 12 months before it becomes one. AI can dramatically improve the frequency and quality of portfolio monitoring by automating the data gathering that currently makes quarterly reviews so time-consuming.

The Early Warning Prompt: "Review the following financial and news data for [BORROWER]. Identify any signals of credit deterioration, covenant risk, or business stress. For each signal identified, rate it Low / Medium / High and suggest the appropriate banker action. Also identify any cross-sell opportunities based on the data."

Run this prompt monthly against each credit in your portfolio using the borrower's latest financials, any public news, and your internal call reports. The output isn't a credit decision — it's a prioritization tool that tells you which credits need your attention this week.

📥

Data Inputs

Pull borrower financials from your core system, recent call reports, covenant compliance certificates, and any public news or filings.

🤖

AI Analysis Pass

Feed each credit's data through your early warning prompt. AI surfaces anomalies, deteriorating ratios, and concerning news in seconds per credit.

🎯

Prioritization Output

AI produces a ranked list: High Priority (call this week), Medium Priority (review next cycle), Low Priority (no action needed).

📞

Banker Action

You focus your time on the High Priority credits. AI has already drafted the call agenda, the borrower briefing, and the questions to ask.

Lesson 4.1

Where Fintech Wins, Where Banks Win

The narrative of "fintech will disrupt banking" has proven to be both right and wrong — right about specific products and customer segments, wrong about the wholesale replacement of the banking relationship. Understanding the precise boundaries of each competitor's advantage is essential for strategy.

High Threat

SMB Payments & Treasury

Fintechs like Brex, Ramp, and Mercury have built vastly superior UI/UX for SMB financial management. Their products are genuinely better on dimensions like onboarding speed, software integrations, and real-time visibility.

Competitors: Brex, Ramp, Mercury, Relay, Novo

High Threat

Small Business Lending

Alternative lenders can approve and fund in 24 hours using cash flow data. For credits under $500K, speed and simplicity often matter more than rate — a race banks are losing by design.

Competitors: Bluevine, Fundbox, Kabbage (AMEX)

Medium Threat

Mid-Market Treasury Services

Treasury management fintechs are encroaching on the $10M–$100M revenue segment, offering API-driven connectivity and automation that legacy TMS platforms don't match. Relationship still matters here, but product gaps are widening.

Competitors: Kyriba, Coupa, Netting platforms

Emerging Threat

Cross-Border Payments

Stablecoins and blockchain-based payment rails are beginning to offer meaningful alternatives to correspondent banking for certain corridors and client types. Still early, but trajectory is clear.

Competitors: Circle (USDC), Stellar, Ripple ODL

Bank Advantage

Complex Credit Relationships

Large, complex credit facilities — syndicated loans, leveraged buyouts, asset-based lending — still require relationship depth, regulatory capital, and the ability to hold risk that fintechs cannot match.

Bank moat: capital, charter, relationships, structure

Bank Advantage

Regulatory Trust & Deposit Insurance

Particularly in uncertain environments, the FDIC guarantee, regulatory oversight, and decades of operational stability matter — especially to CFOs and treasurers managing operating accounts for large businesses.

Bank moat: FDIC, charter, systemic trust, compliance

Lesson 4.2

Buy, Build, or Partner

Every bank faces the same three-way strategic choice when a fintech capability gap is identified. There is no universally correct answer — the right path depends on your bank's size, technical capabilities, timeline, and the strategic importance of the capability in question.

🏗️ Build

Build In-House

Develop the capability internally using your own tech team or through an internal innovation lab.

✓ Full control over IP
✓ Tailored to your specific needs
✓ No vendor dependency
✗ Slowest to market (12–36 months)
✗ Requires deep engineering talent
✗ High ongoing maintenance
🛒 Buy / Acquire

Acquire a Fintech

Purchase an existing fintech company to bring its technology, talent, and customer base in-house.

✓ Immediate capability acquisition
✓ Established technology and team
✓ Eliminates a competitor
✗ Integration complexity is massive
✗ Culture clash destroys value
✗ Expensive (3–10x revenue multiples)
🤝 Partner

Strategic Partnership

Integrate a fintech's product into your bank's offering through a commercial partnership or white-label arrangement.

✓ Fastest to market (3–9 months)
✓ Lower upfront cost
✓ Leverage fintech's ongoing R&D
✗ Vendor concentration risk
✗ Limited customization
✗ Regulatory accountability remains yours
The Decision Framework: For commodity capabilities (payments processing, basic KYC), partner or buy. For differentiated capabilities that define your competitive positioning, build or acquire. For capabilities that are early-stage and untested, partner first — with an option to acquire if the relationship proves strategic.

Lesson 4.3

Open Banking & API Ecosystems

The CFPB's finalization of Section 1033 of the Dodd-Frank Act in 2024 marked a turning point for open banking in the U.S. Banks are now required to make consumer financial data available to authorized third parties through secure APIs — on the customer's request.

For commercial bankers, the implications are significant: your clients' financial data — transaction history, account balances, cash flow patterns — can now flow to fintech competitors with customer consent. This changes the data moat that banks have long relied on.

What It Means for Deposits

When a client's financial data can instantly port to a competing institution, switching costs decline. Relationship depth and service quality become more important than data lock-in.

What It Means for Credit

Fintechs can now access the same cash flow data that your bank sees in your operating accounts — and use it to underwrite the same clients faster and with more sophisticated models.

The Opportunity

Open banking is not only a threat. Banks that build API-friendly infrastructure can embed their products into the software your clients use daily — becoming invisible but essential infrastructure.

The Banker's Response

Deepen relationships beyond the account. Become the advisor who helps clients navigate their full financial ecosystem — not just the institution holding their operating account.

Lesson 4.4 + Synthesis

The Road to 2030

The next five years will see more structural change in commercial banking than the previous twenty. Here is a grounded view of the key developments to watch — and prepare for.

2025–2026

AI Copilots Become Standard Issue

Every major bank deploys AI assistant tools for RMs — loan memo drafting, financial analysis, call prep. The banks that trained their bankers to use these tools well pull ahead. Those that didn't face productivity gaps.

2026–2027

Open Banking Changes the Data Game

Section 1033 compliance is fully in effect. Fintechs are accessing business cash flow data across competing banks. The underwriting data moat is gone for anyone not differentiated by relationship.

2027–2028

Real-Time Everything

FedNow and RTP reach critical mass adoption. Corporate treasury clients expect real-time liquidity visibility and same-day settlement as the default. Banks that can't provide it lose operating account primacy.

2028–2030

Embedded Finance Matures — Banks Choose Their Role

The question for every commercial bank: are you the infrastructure provider (BaaS), the relationship manager (advisor), or the product integrator (fintech aggregator)? Few banks successfully operate all three. This is the strategic choice that will define the next generation of commercial banking franchises.

Module 4 — Key Takeaways

Fintech competition is most acute in SMB banking and payments — less so in complex commercial credit where capital, charter, and relationships are durable moats.
The right fintech response (buy/build/partner) depends on strategic importance and time-to-market requirements — there is no universal answer.
Section 1033 / open banking eliminates data lock-in as a strategy. Relationship depth, advisory value, and product integration quality become the new moats.
Regulatory tightening of fintech (bank partnership rules, BaaS oversight, capital requirements) actually advantages traditional banks in the medium term.
The bankers who will thrive through 2030 are those who combine credit judgment with technology fluency and the ability to advise clients on their own fintech ecosystem.

Lesson 5.3

Automating Excel with Pandas

Pandas is the Python library for working with spreadsheet-like data. Think of it as Excel with superpowers: it can process thousands of rows in milliseconds, run complex calculations automatically, and output formatted reports — with just a few lines of code.

loan_tape_analysis.py
import pandas as pd # Load your loan tape from Excel (or CSV) df = pd.read_excel("loan_tape_Q4.xlsx") # Calculate leverage ratio for every loan in the portfolio df["leverage"] = df["total_debt"] / df["ebitda"] df["dscr"] = df["ebitda"] / (df["interest"] + df["cpltd"]) # Flag credits needing review df["status"] = "OK" df.loc[df["leverage"] > 4.5, "status"] = "REVIEW: High Leverage" df.loc[df["dscr"] < 1.15, "status"] = "REVIEW: Low DSCR" # Pull just the credits needing review watchlist = df[df["status"] != "OK"] # Export to a new Excel file watchlist.to_excel("watchlist_Q4.xlsx", index=False) print(f"Done. {len(watchlist)} credits flagged for review.") # This script runs in under 1 second on a portfolio of 500 loans. # The same task manually in Excel: 2–3 hours.

Analyze the following borrower and produce a concise credit memo narrative. Borrower: {borrower['name']} Industry: {borrower['industry']} Revenue: ${borrower['revenue']:,} EBITDA: ${borrower['ebitda']:,} ({borrower['ebitda_margin']}% margin) Total Debt: ${borrower['total_debt']:,} Leverage: {borrower['leverage']}x Debt/EBITDA DSCR: {borrower['dscr']}x Credit Request: {borrower['request']} Please provide: 1. Borrower Overview (2 sentences) 2. Financial Strengths (3 bullets) 3. Key Risks (3 bullets with mitigants) 4. Preliminary Recommendation (1 sentence) Tone: direct, professional, credit committee-ready. """ # Call the API response = client.messages.create( model="claude-opus-4-6", max_tokens=1024, messages=[{"role": "user", "content": prompt}] ) # Print the output print(response.content[0].text)
